This is not a strange thing, the natural language we use in the real world is very irregular, just need an example to illustrate this. At the same time, this can be used as a reason to explain custom infixes in Haskell why does the operation have to be marked with the symbol “`”. Of course, another obvious reason is the syntax of the Haskell function call (f x y z), infix expressions like this can easily be ambiguous without some special identifiers.

This example is about the incompatibility caused by the precedence of modulo (mod) and exponent (^) operations, even though it is legal in natural language or mathematical expressions (so the human parser is much more powerful than many algorithms).

First let’s examine two common infix operators (binary operators) a × b and a mod b. For parser, their main difference is whether no spaces are allowed on both sides of the operator. In simple terms, this may be nothing, just need to do a special check for the whitespace-eliminated part of “mod” during parse (≥ 1, corresponding to the “+” of the RegExp $\leadsto$ regular expression).

Now, we consider the following definition (exponentiation as part of modulo operation, depending on priority)

  • powExp := unaryExp ('^' unaryExp)*
  • modExp := powExp (' mod ' powExp)*

However, in our implementation, this rule is problematic. powExp will skip all spaces before mod even if the symbol '^' is not present. This results in 'mod' (≠ ' mod ') not being parsed correctly. There are many compromises to solve this problem, such as not introducing pow but directly defining modExp := unaryExp (' mod ' unaryExp)*

Why not design mod to also allow modes without spaces (i.e. 'mod')? Sure, maybe this is the whole answer to this question. This doesn’t cause trouble when you need to match certain identifiers. Although doing so is not syntactically equivalent (say, expressions such as 5mod3 will also be parsed correctly. On the other hand, this also explains why identifiers have many restrictions).
